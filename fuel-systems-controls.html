Compliance is an operations problem, not only a tax problem

Many fuel-related disputes start because the business cannot show a clear chain from purchase to tank to asset to qualifying activity. Spreadsheets and position papers do not fix unmetered dispensing, missing delivery notes or reconciliations that never tie out.

Meridian provides project-based advisory on fuel systems and operational controls for South African mining, quarrying, logistics and industrial operators where fuel volume and evidence complexity are high.

Who this is for

  • Mining and industrial sites with on-site fuel storage and dispensing
  • Businesses preparing for SARS diesel refund audits or verification
  • Operations teams asked to “fix logbooks” after a tax or compliance review
  • Groups consolidating fuel controls across multiple sites or subsidiaries

Receiving and storage controls

Delivery notes, tank dips, custody of fuel on receipt and storage logbooks that trace litres into your system.

Dispensing and metering

Meter discipline, nozzle allocation, asset tagging and usage records tied to equipment—not generic summaries.

Reconciliations and exception reporting

Month-end tie-outs from purchase through consumption, variance thresholds and actionable exception reports.

Asset and site allocation

Registers, equipment lists and site maps that connect diesel use to eligible activities under scrutiny.

Frequently asked questions

Why do fuel controls matter for diesel refund compliance?

SARS tests whether litres claimed match operational reality. Weak dispensing, receiving or reconciliation controls create evidence gaps that undermine diesel refund positions even when the legal interpretation is sound.

What fuel system weaknesses create compliance risk?

Common problems include unmetered dispensing, missing delivery notes, tank dips without reconciliations, unclear asset allocation, manual logbooks that do not tie to source data, and site-level practices that differ from what submissions describe.

Can you help without replacing all hardware?

Yes. Many improvements are process and reporting changes: clearer custody of fuel, better reconciliations, exception reporting and record discipline. Hardware upgrades are recommended only where meters or systems genuinely cannot support defensible records.

How does this connect to data analytics?

Strong controls produce better data. Meridian can advise on compliance-oriented analytics—reconciliations, variance reporting and exception identification—once the underlying fuel journey is mapped.
